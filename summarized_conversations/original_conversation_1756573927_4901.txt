   </div>\n            </div>\n            \n            <div class="content">\n                <h1>Legal Prejudice Analysis Framework</h1>\n                \n                <p class="lead">\n                    The Legal Prejudice Analysis Framework establishes a structured methodology for identifying, assessing, and responding to potential judicial prejudice in legal proceedings. This foundational component provides the definitions, standards, and evaluation criteria based on statutory provisions and key Supreme Court precedents.\n                </p>\n                \n                <div class="alert alert-info">\n                    <i class="fas fa-info-circle"></i>\n                    <strong>Framework Foundation:</strong> This framework is built upon statutory provisions (28 U.S.C. §§ 455, 144) and key Supreme Court precedents including Liteky v. United States and Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.\n                </div>\n                \n                <h2 id="framework-overview">Framework Overview</h2>\n                \n                <p>\n                    The Legal Prejudice Analysis Framework provides a comprehensive approach to evaluating potential judicial prejudice by establishing:\n                </p>\n                \n                <ul>\n                    <li><strong>Clear definitions</strong> of legal prejudice based on statutory provisions and case law</li>\n                    <li><strong>Structured methodology</strong> for identifying and categorizing different types of prejudice</li>\n                    <li><strong>Evaluation criteria</strong> for assessing the severity and impact of potential prejudice</li>\n                    <li><strong>Legal standards</strong> for determining when prejudice rises to the level requiring judicial action</li>\n                    <li><strong>Procedural guidance</strong> for properly documenting and addressing prejudice concerns</li>\n                </ul>\n                \n                <h2 id="framework-components">Framework Components</h2>\n                \n                <p>\n                    The framework consists of three primary components that work together to provide a comprehensive approach to legal prejudice analysis:\n                </p>\n                \n                <div class="card-grid">\n                    <div class="card">\n                        <div class="card-icon">\n                            <i class="fas fa-gavel"></i>\n          <h2 id="prejudice-categories">Prejudice Categories</h2>\n                \n                <p>\n                    The framework categorizes potential judicial prejudice into three main types, each with distinct characteristics and evaluation criteria:\n                </p>\n                \n                <div class="table-responsive">\n                    <table class="table table-bordered">\n                        <thead>\n                            <tr>\n                                <th>Category</th>\n                                <th>Description</th>\n                                <th>Examples</th>\n                                <th>Legal Standards</th>\n                            </tr>\n                        </thead>\n                        <tbody>\n                            <tr>\n                                <td><strong>Relationship-Based Prejudice</strong></td>\n                                <td>Prejudice arising from personal, professional, financial, or other relationships between the judge and parties, attorneys, or witnesses.</td>\n                                <td>\n                                    - Prior representation of a party<br>\n                                    - Financial interest in outcome<br>\n                                    - Family relationship with participant\n                                </td>\n                                <td>\n                                    - 28 U.S.C. § 455(b)(1)-(5)<br>\n                                    - Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.\n                                </td>\n                            </tr>\n                            <tr>\n                                <td><strong>Conduct-Based Prejudice</strong></td>\n                                <td>Prejudice manifested through judicial conduct during proceedings, including statements, rulings, and treatment of participants.</td>\n                                <td>\n                                    - Disparaging remarks about party<br>\n                                    - Consistently adverse rulings<br>\n                                    - Unequal treatment of counsel\n                                </td>\n                                <td>\n                                    - 28 U.S.C. § 455(a)<br>\n                                    - Liteky v. United States\n                                </td>\n                            </tr>\n                            <tr>\n                                <td><strong>Contextual Prejudice</strong></td>\n                                <td>Prejudice arising from the broader context of the case, including public statements, prior involvement in related matters, or extrajudicial knowledge.</td>\n                                <td>\n                                    - Public statements on case issues<br>\n                                    - Prior involvement in related cases<br>\n                                    - Extrajudicial knowledge of facts\n                                </td>\n                                <td>\n                                    - 28 U.S.C. § 455(a), (b)(1)<br>\n                                    - Williams v. Pennsylvania\n                                </td>\n                            </tr>\n                        </tbody>\n                    </table>\n                </div>\n                \n                <h2 id="framework-application">Framework Application</h2>\n                \n                <p>\n                    The Legal Prejudice Analysis Framework is applied through a structured process that integrates with the other components of the system:\n                </p>\n                \n                <ol>\n                    <li>\n                        <strong>Identification</strong> - Recognize potential prejudice factors using the framework\'s categories and definitions.\n                        <ul>\n                            <li>Apply legal standards to identify qualifying factors</li>\n                            <li>Categorize factors by type (relationship, conduct, contextual)</li>\n                            <li>Document initial observations with reference to applicable standards</li>\n                        </ul>\n                    </li>\n                    <li>\n                        <strong>Assessment</strong> - Evaluate identified factors using the <a href="../risk-analysis/index.html">Risk Analysis</a> component.\n                        <ul>\n                            <li>Apply quantitative methods to assess likelihood and impact</li>\n                            <li>Use risk matrices to categorize overall risk level</li>\n                            <li>Apply probability analysis to evaluate strength of evidence</li>\n                        </ul>\n                    </li>\n                    <li>\n                        <strong>Response</strong> - Develop appropriate responses using the <a href="../practical-guide/index.html">Practical Implementation</a> component.\n                        <ul>\n                            <li>Follow the 48-hour triage protocol for immediate action</li>\n                            <li>Select strategic response options based on risk level</li>\n                            <li>Use documentation templates to properly record concerns</li>\n                        </ul>\n                    </li>\n                    <li>\n                        <strong>Validation</strong> - Compare with similar scenarios in the <a href="../case-studies/index.html">Case Studies</a> component.\n                        <ul>\n                            <li>Identify relevant precedent cases with similar factors</li>\n                            <li>Compare assessment and response strategies</li>\n                            <li>Refine approach based on documented outcomes</li>\n                        </ul>\n                    </li>\n                </ol>\n                \n                <h2 id="integration-with-other-components">Integration with Other Components</h2>\n                \n                <p>\n                    The Legal Prejudice Analysis Framework serves as the foundation for the entire system, with direct connections to all other components:\n                </p>\n                \n                <div class="framework-diagram">\n                    <img src="../assets/images/framework-integration.png" alt="Framework Integration Diagram" class="img-fluid">\n                </div>\n                \n                <ul>\n                    <li>\n                        <strong>Risk Analysis</strong> - Disqualification of justice, judge, or magistrate judge\n                        <ul>\n                            <li>§ 455(a) - General standard requiring recusal "in any proceeding in which his impartiality might reasonably be questioned"</li>\n                            <li>§ 455(b) - Specific circumstances requiring recusal, including personal bias, prior involvement, financial interest, and relationships</li>\n                        </ul>\n                    </li>\n                    <li>\n                        <strong>28 U.S.C. § 144</strong> - Bias or prejudice of judge\n                        <ul>\n                            <li>Procedure for filing affidavit alleging personal bias or prejudice</li>\n                            <li>Requirements for timeliness, specificity, and good faith</li>\n                        </ul>\n                    </li>\n                </ul>\n                \n                <h3>Supreme Court Precedents</h3>\n                \n                <ul>\n                    <li>\n                        <strong>Liteky v.
